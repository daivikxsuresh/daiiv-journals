July 2025 — Manufacturing's New Map

As reshoring accelerates and tariffs reshape sourcing economics, understanding manufacturing types and supplier relationships is more valuable than ever. This month we break down the manufacturing landscape and explore the equities best positioned to capitalize on the domestic manufacturing renaissance.

The United States is in the early innings of the largest domestic manufacturing expansion in a generation. Semiconductor fabs, EV battery plants, pharmaceutical API facilities, and defense production lines are being built or expanded at a pace not seen since World War II. Understanding the different types of manufacturing — and the supplier ecosystems that support them — is foundational knowledge for anyone navigating this landscape.

Manufacturing Types
Discrete, process, and hybrid — each demands different inventory, quality, and tracking approaches

Manufacturing Types — Know Your Options

Discrete Manufacturing

Produces distinct, countable items: cars, electronics, furniture, machinery. Each unit is identifiable and can be tracked individually through the production process. Examples: automotive plants, electronics assembly lines, aerospace manufacturing facilities. Characterized by complex bills of materials (BOMs) — sometimes thousands of components per finished unit — multiple production stages, significant supplier coordination requirements, and detailed work-in-process (WIP) tracking. Automotive manufacturing is the archetype: a modern vehicle contains 30,000+ parts from hundreds of suppliers across dozens of countries.

Process Manufacturing

Produces goods in bulk, often by mixing, separating, heating, or chemically transforming raw materials: chemicals, food and beverage, pharmaceuticals, petroleum refining, plastics. The output is often continuous — a liquid, gas, or granular material — rather than individual countable units. Inventory and quality management look fundamentally different from discrete manufacturing: you measure by weight, volume, or concentration rather than unit count. Batch traceability and regulatory compliance (especially in pharma and food) are paramount concerns.

Hybrid Manufacturing

Combines discrete and process elements — the most common model in complex modern supply chains. A pharmaceutical company might use process manufacturing to produce active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs), then discrete manufacturing for tablet pressing, coating, and packaging. A food company uses process manufacturing for sauce production, then discrete manufacturing for filling, labeling, and case packing. Most complex modern supply chains involve hybrid approaches, and the systems and metrics for managing each component must account for these differences.

Supplier Strategy
Transactional vs. strategic suppliers — reshoring forces rebuilding domestic networks after 30 years

Supplier Relationships — The Strategic Layer

Supply chains are only as strong as their supplier relationships. How you structure and manage these relationships determines your flexibility, cost position, and resilience. The reshoring movement has made this more complex: companies must simultaneously manage established offshore supplier relationships while building new domestic ones — often with suppliers that don't yet have the scale or process maturity of their overseas counterparts.

Transactional vs. Strategic Suppliers

  • Transactional suppliers: commodity components, multiple qualified alternatives, price-driven selection — managed at arm's length with competitive bidding
  • Strategic suppliers: unique capabilities, deep technical integration, relationship-driven — require investment in the partnership, not just the contract
  • Preferred suppliers: middle ground — certified performance record, preferred status for new business, but not sole-sourced; competitive enough to maintain accountability

Supplier Risk Management

  • Single-source risk: one supplier failure cripples your production — the 2011 Japan earthquake and tsunami shut down Toyota's global production because of single-source components
  • Geographic concentration risk: all suppliers in one region are exposed to the same natural disasters, political events, or port disruptions simultaneously
  • Financial health monitoring: a supplier bankruptcy creates immediate supply chain crisis — monitoring supplier financial health is as important as monitoring quality
  • Cyber risk: supplier systems connected to yours are attack vectors — the SolarWinds breach showed how supplier access can compromise the entire customer network

The reshoring trend is forcing companies to rebuild domestic supplier networks that atrophied over 30 years of offshoring. This takes time — supplier qualification, capacity development, and process alignment don't happen overnight. The companies winning today are those that started building domestic supplier relationships 2-3 years ago. The window to get ahead of this curve is narrowing.

"The reshoring movement isn't just a policy trend — it's a structural shift in how America makes things. Position accordingly."

Equities to Watch
CAT, ETN, PCAR, AME, ROK, NVDA — direct structural exposure to the manufacturing buildout

Equities to Watch — The Reshoring Trade

The domestic manufacturing renaissance creates investable themes across multiple sectors. These aren't speculative bets — they're companies with direct, structural exposure to the trends reshaping where and how America makes things. Each represents a different dimension of the manufacturing supply chain buildout:

  • $CAT (Caterpillar) — infrastructure and construction equipment for new manufacturing facilities, ports, and logistics infrastructure
  • $ETN (Eaton) — electrical systems, power management, and switchgear for new manufacturing plants requiring significant electrical infrastructure
  • $PCAR (PACCAR) — commercial trucks for growing domestic freight volumes as reshored production generates more intra-U.S. logistics activity
  • $AME (AMETEK) — specialized electronic instruments and electromechanical devices for advanced manufacturing quality control and automation
  • $ROK (Rockwell Automation) — factory automation, industrial control systems, and smart manufacturing platforms for reshored production facilities
  • $NVDA (NVIDIA) — AI hardware enabling smart manufacturing, digital twins, computer vision quality control, and autonomous logistics

"The reshoring movement isn't just a policy trend — it's a structural shift in how America makes things. Position accordingly."
